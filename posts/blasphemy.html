  • Hadith & Fiqh: Introduced worldly punishments, including death, especially for insulting the Prophet.

Blasphemy in Hindu Tradition

Hinduism is pluralistic, with no single scripture or authority prescribing punishments for speech against gods or scriptures. Let‚Äôs break it down:

1. Vedas and Upanishads

  • These oldest texts don‚Äôt mention blasphemy as a crime.
  • Questioning, doubting, or even denying gods is part of the philosophical tradition. For example, the Nasadiya Sukta (Rigveda 10.129) openly questions the existence and knowledge of the gods themselves.

üëâ So skepticism was not seen as punishable; rather, inquiry was encouraged.

2. Epics & Puranas

  • Many characters openly insult or mock gods (like Ravana mocking Rama, Shishupala mocking Krishna).
  • The gods respond in the narrative, but there is no religious law saying society must punish blasphemers.

üëâ This shows Hindu tradition tolerates even sharp criticism of religion.

3. Dharmashastra (like Manusmriti)

  • These texts give social/religious rules, but not really laws on blasphemy in the Abrahamic sense.
  • Speaking ill of gods is seen as adharma (sin), but punishment is mostly karmic (fate after death), not state-legal punishment.

4. Bhakti & Sant tradition

  • Medieval Bhakti saints (like Kabir, Ravidas, Mirabai) often criticized rituals, priests, and even gods in strong language.
  • Instead of being executed as blasphemers, they were revered by many and started reform movements.

üëâ This shows Hindu tradition tolerates even sharp criticism of religion.

5. Today

  • Hindu-majority India historically never had blasphemy laws.
  • Modern Indian law (Section 295A IPC), which punishes ‚Äúinsult to religion,‚Äù was introduced by the British in 1927, not by Hindu scripture.

‚ú® Summary

  • Hinduism does not have a concept of blasphemy as a crime.
  • Criticizing gods or scriptures is seen as disrespectful or sinful but not punishable by law in the tradition.
  • The idea of blasphemy laws came to India via colonial rule, not from Vedas, Puranas, or Bhakti teachings.

Blasphemy in Guru Granth Sahib

1. No concept of ‚Äúblasphemy law‚Äù

The Guru Granth Sahib does not prescribe any worldly punishment for people who insult God, Gurus, or the scripture. Instead, it emphasizes that disrespect only harms the one who shows it, because God cannot be diminished.

2. What it actually says

Mocking God or the Guru is seen as ignorance (ahamkar, haumai), not as a crime. Example ideas:

  • A person who insults the Guru or God is simply lost in ego; their soul suffers separation from divine truth.
  • God is beyond harm ‚Äî human words can‚Äôt ‚Äúhurt‚Äù the Infinite.

3. Sikh Gurus‚Äô approach in history

The Sikh Gurus themselves faced ridicule and persecution (e.g., Guru Nanak was mocked for rejecting rituals, Guru Arjan and Guru Tegh Bahadur were executed by Mughal rulers for their stance). Their response was:

  • Patience, forgiveness, and courage, not revenge.
  • Sikh history shows the idea of standing firm in faith, not punishing others for disbelief or insult.

4. Focus on inner discipline

The scripture teaches:

  • Criticism and insults from others should be met with humility (sehaj, shanti).
  • The real ‚Äúblasphemy‚Äù in Sikh thought is forgetting God, living in ego, or exploiting others, not insulting words.

5. Today

  • Sikhism doesn‚Äôt have religious blasphemy laws.
  • However, socially, many Sikhs consider desecration of Guru Granth Sahib (like burning or tearing it) deeply offensive, since the scripture is revered as the living Guru.

‚ú® Summary (Sikh view):

  • Guru Granth Sahib: Insults do not harm God, only the insulter.
  • No punishment system, only karmic consequences.
  • True disrespect is forgetting God, not mere words.

Summary of Religions

1. Religions that do not prescribe worldly punishment for blasphemy in scripture

  • Hinduism: Vedas, Upanishads, Puranas ‚Äì No law punishing criticism or insult to gods; disrespect is a sin or bad karma, not a crime.
  • Sikhism (Guru Granth Sahib): Mocking God or scripture is spiritually wrong, but the ‚Äúpunishment‚Äù is personal or karmic; no legal or violent action is prescribed.
  • Some Buddhist traditions: Insulting the Buddha or monks is ethically wrong, but there‚Äôs no divine or legal punishment mandated in scripture.

‚úÖ These religions focus on inner consequences, spiritual discipline, and moral conduct, not executing or imprisoning someone for blasphemy.

2. Religions that include punishable blasphemy in religious law (not always in scripture)

  • Islam: Qur‚Äôan itself ‚Äì no earthly punishment; Hadith and later Islamic law introduced death/penalties for insulting God/Prophet.
  • Christianity: Early medieval canon law treated blasphemy as a crime; modern secular states largely removed it.
  • Judaism: Torah prescribes severe punishment (death) for certain blasphemy in the Hebrew Bible, though in practice ancient Jewish courts rarely executed it.

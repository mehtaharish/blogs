Introduction

Karwa Chauth is a North Indian festival observed by married women who fast from sunrise to moonrise for the long life and well-being of their husbands. The term Karwa refers to a clay pot used during the ritual, while Chauth means the fourth day of the waning moon in the Hindu lunar month of Kartik.

Although the festival carries deep cultural and emotional value, it does not appear in the Vedas or major Pur─Бс╣Зas. Ancient texts only describe general moon worship (Chandra or Soma) and various vratas (fasts), but none called Karwa Chauth. The practice as known today emerged much later, likely in medieval North India, where local communities combined lunar reverence, Parvati-Shiva legends, and regional customs into a single devotional observance.

    Few attributes of this festival

  • Day: The fourth day of the waning phase of the Kartik month (usually in October-November). The waning phase тАФ from full moon to the next new moon.
  • Fast: A waterless fast (no eating, no drinking) from sunrise to moonrise.
  • Worship: In the evening, worship of the Karwa (clay pot) and the moon is performed.
  • Conclusion: The fast is broken after seeing the moon and drinking water from the husband's hand.

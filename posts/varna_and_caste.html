Varna and Caste ‚Äî A Chronological Transformation

From flexible philosophy to rigid birth-based system

The terms varna and caste are often used interchangeably today, but historically they refer to very different concepts.

Understanding how a flexible philosophical idea slowly transformed into a rigid birth-based system is essential to understanding casteism in India.

This article traces that transformation chronologically, using references from early Indian texts.

Phase 1: Cosmic and Philosophical Origin of Varna

Early Vedic Period (~1500‚Äì1000 BCE)

The earliest discussion of varna appears in the Rigveda, specifically in the Purusha Sukta (Rigveda 10.90).

Purusha Sukta ‚Äî Rigveda 10.90

Purusha is described as:

  • All-pervading
  • Present within creation and beyond it
  • The source from which the universe unfolds

This clearly establishes Purusha as a cosmic principle, not a human being.

Phase 2: Ethical Clarification of Varna

Upanishadic & Gita Period (~800‚Äì300 BCE)

  • Varna is not inherited
  • A person‚Äôs role can change with conduct and inclination
  • Birth is not the determining factor

At this stage

  • Varna is fluid
  • Ethical and psychological, not legal
  • Closely tied to personal responsibility

Phase 3: Social Regulation Begins

Dharmasutra Period (~500‚Äì200 BCE)

As society becomes larger and more complex, codes of conduct emerge to guide everyday life.

  • These texts aim to regulate social behavior, not philosophy
  • Varna begins to acquire social rigidity
  • However, it is still not fully hereditary
  • These rules function as social norms, not divine law
This phase marks a transition, not casteism yet.

Phase 4: Birth-Based Hierarchy Takes Shape

Smriti Period (~200 BCE ‚Äì 200 CE)

This is the decisive turning point. Texts such as the Manusmriti fundamentally alter the meaning of varna.

‚ÄúIn the Smritis, social rules are elevated toward sacred obligation.‚Äù

Key changes introduced

  • Varna becomes birth-based
  • Brahmins are declared superior
  • Different punishments for the same crime based on varna
  • Social mobility is restricted
  • Inter-varna mixing is condemned

Most importantly

Social rules are presented as religious obligations. This converts social regulation into sacred law, making disobedience a moral and religious offense.

This is where casteism begins.

Phase 5: Expansion into the Jati System

Puranic Period (~300‚Äì1000 CE)

During this period:

  • Society fragments into thousands of jati (birth groups)
  • Occupations become hereditary
  • Marriage is restricted strictly within jati
  • Violations are framed as sin (paap) with spiritual consequences
Varna becomes largely theoretical. Jati becomes lived reality.

Caste is now embedded in:

  • Daily life
  • Social identity
  • Religious fear

Phase 6: Philosophical Challenge Without Social Reform

Bhakti & Vedantic Period (~600‚Äì1500 CE)

Bhakti movements

  • Emphasize spiritual equality
  • Allow devotion regardless of caste
  • Do not dismantle caste in social practice

Advaita Vedanta

  • Asserts non-duality and oneness
  • Rejects all distinctions at the ultimate level
  • Allows caste to persist at the social (vyavahƒÅrika) level

Thus

  • Philosophically, caste loses justification
  • Socially, it survives through inertia
Philosophically, caste loses justification; socially, it survives.

Phase 7: Modern Period

Colonial & Post-Independence India

  • Legal equality is established
  • Caste discrimination is constitutionally abolished
  • Yet caste survives socially:
  • Marriage
  • Politics
  • Networks
  • Identity
Laws change faster than social consciousness.

Conclusion

Casteism in India did not originate in early Vedic philosophy. It emerged gradually when:

  • Functional roles became hereditary
  • Social rules were sanctified as religious law
  • Fear of sin replaced ethical reasoning

Understanding this historical process allows a clearer distinction between philosophical varna and social caste ‚Äî and shows that caste was constructed over time, not divinely ordained.

Caste was constructed historically, not divinely ordained.

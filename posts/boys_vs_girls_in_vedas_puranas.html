Boy vs Girl in Core Vedas and Post-Vedic Texts

📖 Note: This article is intended for reflection and sharing knowledge. It is not written for debate, but for thoughtful exchange of perspectives.

Introduction

The core Vedas (Śruti), including the Ṛgveda, Sāmaveda, Yajurveda, Atharvaveda, and their associated Saṁhitās, Brāhmaṇas, Āraṇyakas, and Upaniṣads, present a largely gender-neutral worldview. In these texts, girls and boys are equally celebrated in spiritual, educational, and ritual contexts. Women seers like Lopāmudrā, Gārgī, and Maitreyī composed hymns, engaged in philosophical debates, and were recognized for their wisdom, showing that freedom of speech, education, and participation in rituals were open to both genders. Similarly, rites related to birth, marriage, property, cremation, and ancestral offerings in the Vedas either include daughters explicitly or do not differentiate by gender.

In contrast, Post-Vedic texts (Smṛtis and Purāṇas) such as Manusmṛti, Yājñavalkya Smṛti, and the Purāṇas introduced social and ritual hierarchies that favored boys over girls. Sons became the primary heirs of property, the main performers of śrāddha and cremation rituals, and the preferred carriers of lineage. Girls, while still valued, were often restricted to secondary roles, their rights limited mainly to strī-dhan (dowry or personal gifts), and their participation in public rituals and education curtailed. Even in marriage, daughters’ choices were increasingly controlled by guardians, while sons retained greater freedom. Practices like Sati, glorified in later Purāṇas, further demonstrate the patriarchal tilt in post-Vedic society.

In essence, while the Vedas uphold equality and inclusion, later texts progressively institutionalized gender-based restrictions, highlighting a significant shift in the treatment and social roles of boys and girls.

Let us explore this topic in greater detail by examining various aspects individually.

📜 Definition of Vedic and Post-Vedic Texts

The Vedic texts (Śruti) are the oldest and most sacred Hindu scriptures, believed to be divinely revealed. They include the four Vedas — Ṛgveda, Sāmaveda, Yajurveda, and Atharvaveda — along with their Saṁhitās, Brāhmaṇas, Āraṇyakas, and Upaniṣads. These texts focus on hymns, rituals, philosophy, and guidance for sacrifices, promoting spiritual knowledge, ethical living, and societal harmony, generally without gender bias. Post-Vedic texts (Smṛti and Purāṇas), on the other hand, are human-authored works that interpret and apply Vedic teachings. They include law codes (like Manusmṛti), epics (Mahābhārata, Rāmāyaṇa), and Purāṇas (Vishnu, Padma, Garuḍa, etc.), offering guidance on law, social norms, rituals, and mythology. These later texts are more context-specific and often show a patriarchal tilt, especially in matters like inheritance, ritual duties, and gender roles.

  • Core Vedas (Śruti)
    • Period: c. 1500 – 1000 BCE (Rigveda earliest hymns), up to c. 800 BCE (Upaniṣads).
    • Includes:
      • Saṁhitā (hymn collections)
      • Brāhmaṇa (ritual explanation texts)
      • Āraṇyaka (forest texts, meditative rituals)
      • Upaniṣad (philosophy, knowledge of Brahman)
    • Status: Śruti (divine revelation, eternal sound).
    • View on girls: Birth of daughters not condemned; women sages exist; sons preferred for ritual continuity, but daughters also blessed.
  • Gṛhya Sūtras (Kalpa-sūtra literature)
    • Period: c. 600 – 200 BCE.
    • Nature: Later ritual manuals based on Vedic mantras.
    • Focus: Household rituals — marriage, birth ceremonies, funerals, ancestor offerings (śrāddha).
    • Status: Not śruti; considered auxiliary texts (Vedāṅga Kalpa).
    • View on girls: Start of stronger emphasis that eldest son performs funeral rites.
  • Dharmaśāstras (e.g., Manusmṛti, Yājñavalkya-smṛti)
    • Period: c. 200 BCE – 500 CE.
    • Nature: Law and ethics codes.
    • Focus: Duties (dharma), caste rules, inheritance, conduct, gender norms.
    • Status: Smṛti (remembered, humanly composed).
    • View on girls: Strong son-preference. Sons seen as necessary for ancestor rites; daughters treated as “given away” (kanyādāna).
  • Smṛti Literature in General
    • Period: c. 500 BCE – 1200 CE (ongoing expansions even later).
    • Includes: Epics (Mahābhārata, Rāmāyaṇa), Purāṇas, Dharmaśāstras, Nīti-texts.
    • Status: Smṛti (respected but below Veda).
    • Social Practices:
      • Only son performing śrāddha becomes norm.
      • Daughter seen as Lakṣmī (auspicious), but socially “parāya dhan” (someone else's wealth).
      • Purāṇas glorify divine feminine (Devi), but everyday custom became patriarchal.

Girls’ Birth

🌿 Core Vedas (Śruti)

In the Vedas, prayers for sons (putra) are more common, but this does not mean that daughters (putrī) were considered a curse. Some key points:

  • Atharvaveda 6.11.3: “A heroic son may be born to us.”
    👉 Here, the prayer is for a son because sons were considered responsible for continuing śrāddha (ancestor rituals) and the lineage.
  • Rigveda 10.85.46 (Vivāha Sūkta):
    “May you be the mother of heroic sons, and may your daughter-in-law bring prosperity into your home.”
    👉 Here, the role of daughters is described as auspicious and compared to wealth and prosperity (Lakṣmī).
  • Rigveda 10.159 (Apālā hymn):
    The female seer (ṛṣikā) Apālā expresses her life and experiences — this shows that the birth of women was given due respect in the Vedic worldview.

🔑 Summary of Vedic attitude:

  • Preference for sons was due to ritualistic reasons.
  • However, the birth of daughters was never considered inauspicious or unfortunate.
  • In the Vedas, women were regarded as Vāk-devī (goddess of speech) and Gṛha-lakṣmī (goddess of the household).

📜 Purāṇas and Later Smṛti Texts

By the time of the Purāṇas and Dharmaśāstras (c. 200 BCE – 1200 CE), the preference for sons became even stronger.

  • Manusmṛti 9.135–138: Sons were made mandatory for repaying the debt to ancestors (pitr̥-ṛṇa).
    👉 This is why daughters were referred to as “parāya dhan” (someone else’s wealth, to be given away in marriage).
  • Garuda Purāṇa (Pretakalpa): Funeral and śrāddha rites were almost always performed by sons.
    👉 The role of daughters was excluded here.
  • Vishnu Purāṇa & Padma Purāṇa: Daughters were considered divine (devī-svarūp), but they were also seen as gifts to be given away in marriage (kanyādāna = the greatest gift).
  • Bhāgavata Purāṇa: In several instances, daughters were portrayed as embodiments of devotion (e.g., stories of Devahūti, Kunti, and Draupadī).

🔑 Summary of Purāṇic attitude:

  • The birth of girls was referred to as “Gṛha-lakṣmī” (goddess of the household).
  • However, rituals (like śrāddha and lineage continuity) became strictly son-centric.
  • In Purāṇic stories, daughters were glorified as divine figures, but in social practice, they were seen as “parāya dhan” (someone else’s wealth), while sons were considered the primary heirs.

⚖️ Contrast

  • Core Vedas (Śruti): Daughters were considered auspicious, not cursed; sons were preferred for ritual duties, but women had spiritual authority and even composed hymns.
  • Purāṇas/Smṛti: Daughters were seen as Lakṣmī (goddess of wealth), but socially “given away”; sons were made ritually indispensable, creating a stronger preference for boys.

Education & Women’s Role

🌿 In Core Vedas (Śruti)

The Vedas themselves do not explicitly restrict education by gender. Instead, they show examples of women actively learning, teaching, and even composing hymns.

  • Women Ṛṣikās (female seers):
    Around 27 women seers are credited with composing Vedic hymns (e.g., Lopāmudrā, Ghoṣā, Apālā, Maitreyī, Gargī). Their hymns are preserved in the Ṛgveda.
    → This shows that women could be authors of revelation.
  • Debates & philosophy:
    • Gārgī Vācaknavī in the Bṛhadāraṇyaka Upaniṣad debates Yājñavalkya on metaphysics.
    • Maitreyī, wife of Yājñavalkya, is taught ātma-vidyā (knowledge of the Self).
    These stories prove women were considered fit for the highest knowledge (Brahmavidyā).
  • Sanskāras (rites of passage):
    Girls could undergo upanayana (sacred thread initiation) in early Vedic times. Such girls were called Brahmavādinī (lifelong student of Veda) or Sadyovadhū (who studied until marriage).

🔑 Conclusion from Śruti:
The Vedas see women as capable of Vedic study, debate, and spiritual realization, with examples of women sages, philosophers, and teachers.

📜 In Purāṇas & Later Dharma Texts

By the time of Purāṇas (and also Dharmaśāstras like Manusmṛti), the view of women’s role shifts.

  • Restricted Vedic education:
    Later texts often say Vedas should not be taught to women. Manu, for example, suggests women should be instructed in dharma through fathers/husbands, not through direct Vedic study.
  • Role centered on domesticity:
    Women are praised primarily as wives and mothers. Their religious merit is linked to serving the husband, raising children, and devotion.
  • Purāṇic stories:
    • Many Purāṇas glorify sati-like devotion (e.g., Sāvitrī reviving Satyavān).
    • Women are celebrated more as symbols of śakti (divine energy), but not usually encouraged in independent scholarship.

🔑 Conclusion from Purāṇas:
The Purāṇic worldview tends to limit women’s direct participation in Vedic study, framing their role more in terms of household duty and devotion rather than open intellectual debate.

⚖️ Contrast

  • Śruti (Vedas/Upaniṣads): Women = sages, students, philosophers. Education open.
  • Purāṇas/Dharma texts: Women = wives, mothers, devotees. Education restricted, roles narrowed.

✅ Summary of Sources

  • Ṛgveda hymns: RV 1.179 (Lopāmudrā), RV 8.91 (Apālā), RV 5.28 (Viśvavārā), RV 10.39–40 (Ghoṣā), RV 10.75 (Nadī-stuti, female involvement).
  • Upaniṣads: Bṛhadāraṇyaka Upaniṣad 2.4, 3.6, 3.8, 4.5 (Maitreyī & Gārgī).
  • Later texts: Manusmṛti, Purāṇas, epics → restrict women’s Vedic study.

ŚRĀDDHA

🌿 Śruti (Core Vedas: Saṁhitā + Brāhmaṇa + Āraṇyaka + Upaniṣad)

  1. Ṛgveda 10.15 (Pitṛ Sūkta)
    • Ancestors (Pitṛs) are worshipped and invoked with soma and food offerings.
    • However, there is no gender-specific requirement mentioned (e.g., only sons can perform).
    • The mantra includes everyone who performs the yajña (sacrifice) and oblations.
  2. Yajurveda Saṁhitā (19.43–49)
    • Mentions offerings of water, food, and sesame seeds to ancestors.
    • Again, there is no clause restricting the performance to sons only.
  3. Śatapatha Brāhmaṇa (11.6.2)
    • Oblations are offered to ancestors through fire.
    • The performer is generally the householder (gṛhastha yajamāna), but this is not exclusive to males.
  4. Upaniṣads (Chāndogya 5.10.3–4)
    • The "Pitṛyāṇa path" (ancestors’ path) is described, but there is no mention of gender-specific roles in the rituals.

👉 Summary of Śruti:

  • Offerings to ancestors are universal.
  • No explicit differentiation like “only sons can perform” or “daughters cannot perform.”
  • The concept of excluding girls is not found here.

📜 Smṛti / Purāṇa Traditions (later development)

  1. Gṛhya Sūtras (600–200 BCE)
    • Śrāddha rituals became structured during this period.
    • Implicit assumption: the householder male (son or husband) performs the rituals.
  2. Manusmṛti (9.137)
    • Famous line: “Through a son he conquers the worlds, through a son’s son he obtains immortality, but through the son of a son’s son he ascends to the sun.”
    • Thus, sons were made necessary for the salvation of ancestors (pitṛ-mokṣa).
    • Daughters were excluded from performing śrāddha rituals.
  3. Mahābhārata (Ādi Parva 74.40, Anuśāsana Parva 106)
    • The birth of a son is described as essential for repaying the “debt to ancestors” (pitr̥-ṛṇa).
    • However, some versions mention that the son of a daughter (putrikā-putra) can also perform the rituals if there is no direct son.
  4. Garuḍa Purāṇa (Pretakalpa)
    • Śrāddha is fully codified with piṇḍa-dāna (offering of rice balls), tarpana (water offerings), and pitṛ-pakṣa (ancestor fortnight).
    • It states that if there is no son, agnate male relatives or, ultimately, the daughter’s son (putrikā-putra) can perform the rituals.
    • Daughters themselves do not perform the rituals.

⚖️ Contrast

  • Core Vedas (Śruti): No strict male-only rule. Offerings to ancestors are open to the yajamāna (performer), with no explicit gender restrictions.
  • Smṛti & Purāṇas: A strong preference for sons as the sole performers of śrāddha. Daughters were excluded, and their sons (putrikā-putra) were occasionally allowed as substitutes. This discrimination of “boys > girls” developed outside the Śruti, in the Smṛti/Purāṇic era.

Cremation Rituals

🌿 Śruti (Core Vedas – Saṁhitā, Brāhmaṇa, Āraṇyaka, Upaniṣad)

  1. Ṛgveda 10.16 – Antyeṣṭi Sūkta (Cremation hymn)
    • Hymn recited at cremation: “Let the fire not consume your skin, let it not consume your body. Let your eye go to the sun, your prāṇa to the wind, your soul to the waters, your body to the plants.”
    • Here, there is no gender restriction – it is addressed to the deceased person in general (male or female).
  2. Atharvaveda 18.2–3
    • Mantras for the deceased: “Go forth, go forth on those ancient paths where our forefathers have gone before.”
    • Again, gender-neutral.
  3. Śatapatha Brāhmaṇa (13.8.1–2)
    • Agni (fire) is described as the conveyor of the soul to ancestors and gods.
    • The performer is usually the agnidhr̥ (fire tender) or yajamāna (householder), but there is no explicit restriction that it must be a son.
  4. Upaniṣads (Kaṭha Up. 1.1–1.2, Bṛhadāraṇyaka 3.2)
    • The focus is on the afterlife, the ātman (soul), and the paths of ancestors (pitṛyāṇa) or gods (devayāṇa), not on gender-specific ritual performers.

👉 Summary of Śruti:

  • Cremation rituals are clearly described.
  • Mantras and rites apply to both men and women.
  • The performer is the yajamāna (householder), who could be male or female, with no explicit restrictions.
  • There is no distinction between boys and girls in this context.

📜 Smṛti & Purāṇa Traditions (later)

  1. Gṛhya Sūtras (600–200 BCE)
    • Cremation (antyeṣṭi) was structured as part of household dharma.
    • Implicit assumption: the eldest son lights the funeral pyre.
    • Daughters were not mentioned as performers.
  2. Manusmṛti (5.59, 9.137)
    • The son (putra) is described as the “deliverer from naraka” (hell), essential for ensuring peace in the afterlife.
    • Hence, funeral rites were linked to the son.
    • Girls were excluded from performing funeral duties.
  3. Mahābhārata (Anuśāsana Parva 146.24)
    • It is stated: “A sonless man goes to darkness; the son leads the father to heaven.”
    • However, in some instances, it is mentioned that if there is no son, the daughter’s son (putrikā-putra) or a male relative can perform the rituals.
  4. Purāṇas (Garuda Purāṇa, Agni Purāṇa, Matsya Purāṇa)
    • The Garuḍa Purāṇa (Pretakalpa) details that it is the son’s duty to light the pyre and perform piṇḍa-dāna (offering of rice balls).
    • For women: the same cremation mantras apply, but the performer is always a male relative (son, husband, or brother).
    • Daughters were not directly given the authority to perform these rituals.

⚖️ Contrast

  • Core Vedas (Śruti): Cremation hymns (RV 10.16, AV 18.2) are universal. There is no explicit rule that “only sons must perform.” Girls are not excluded – the framework is neutral.
  • Later Smṛti & Purāṇas: Sons (putra) were declared essential. “Lighting the pyre” and “śrāddha after cremation” were tied to sons only. Girls were excluded as ritual performers, with their sons (putrikā-putra) occasionally allowed as substitutes.

Wealth Right

🌿 Core Vedas (Śruti)

  • Recognition of daughters in the Rigveda: Daughters were regarded with wealth, prosperity, and blessings.
  • Ṛgveda 10.85.46: “May this bride be the queen of her household, ruling over the property, cattle, and home.”
    👉 Here, the daughter is referred to as the Lakṣmī (goddess of wealth) of the household, and she is given rights over property.
  • Atharvaveda 14.1.20: The bride is considered the owner of prosperity and property in the household.
  • There are no direct inheritance rules (explicit laws) in the Vedas. Wealth rights in the Vedas were not restricted based on gender; however, later Dharmasūtras and Smṛti texts introduced specific rules.
  • Daughter’s position: Some hymns describe daughters as contributors to prosperity, wealth, and family growth. There is no clear discrimination between boys and girls in this context.

📜 Purāṇas & Later Smṛti Texts

  • Manusmṛti (9.130–131): Sons were considered the heirs of the household, and property rights were primarily given to them.
    “A son alone can save the ancestors from hell (putra-narakāt trāyate iti putraḥ).”
    — This led to the inheritance system becoming patrilineal.
  • Yājñavalkya Smṛti (2.117–123): Daughters were granted inheritance rights only if there were no sons.
  • Purāṇas (Garuda Purāṇa, Vishnu Purāṇa, etc.): Symbolically, daughters were given wealth and strī-dhan (jewels, gifts, dowry), but sons were preferred for primary property inheritance.
  • Strī-dhan Concept: In Smṛti and Purāṇas, daughters were given full rights over strī-dhan (jewels, ornaments, gifts at marriage, maternal gifts). This was considered their personal property, which neither the husband nor the in-laws could claim.

⚖️ Contrast

  • Vedas (Śruti): There was no explicit gender bias in wealth rights. Brides/daughters were referred to as “owners of property” and “queens of the household.” Both sons and daughters were associated with divine blessings and prosperity.
  • Purāṇas / Smṛtis: Sons → primary heirs of property and lineage. Daughters → inheritance rights only if there were no sons, but full rights over strī-dhan. The practical system became patrilineal, with a clear preference for boys.

Marriage Right

🌿 Core Vedas (Śruti)

  • In the Vedas (Rigveda, Atharvaveda), marriage is considered a sacred bond based on mutual consent and prayers from both sides.
  • The rights and choices of the bride:
    • Ṛgveda 10.27.12: The bride herself rejects the groom: “I am not accepting this man; he is not my choice.” → This is direct evidence that the bride had the right to choose.
    • Atharvaveda 7.38.4–6: The bride had the right to choose her groom according to her own will (similar to the concept of swayamvara).
  • The role of the groom:
    • For the groom, obtaining a spouse was considered a duty, but it could not be fulfilled without the consent and choice of the bride.
    • Ṛgveda 10.85 (Vivāh Sūkta): Both the groom and bride are described as sahadharmacarī (equal partners) in marriage.

➡️ Thus, in the Śruti, both sons and daughters had the right to marriage, and the bride’s choice was clearly highlighted.

📜 Purāṇas & Later Smṛti Literature

  • Manusmṛti 9.3: “A girl should be given in marriage by her father, while she is still young, to a suitable man.” → Here, the bride’s choice was reduced, and control by guardians increased.
  • 8 types of marriage (Manusmṛti 3.21–34, Purāṇic elaboration):
    • Brahma, Daiva, Ārṣa, Prajāpatya (mostly arranged by guardians)
    • Gandharva (love/swayamvara style — mutual choice of the bride and groom)
    • Rākṣasa, Āsura, Paiśāca (forceful/condemned types).
    Gandharva marriage (mutual choice) was acknowledged in the scriptures, but later it was discouraged and labeled as “secondary.”
  • Purāṇas (Padma, Skanda, etc.): Marriage was made a duty of the father/guardian (pitr̥-dharma), where it became their responsibility to arrange the daughter’s marriage. Boys were given more freedom to choose their spouse.

➡️ Thus, in the Purāṇas and Smṛti, marriage rights became gendered:

  • Boys: Had more freedom to choose their spouse and marry according to their will.
  • Girls: Were placed under the control of guardians, and their choice became secondary (with the exception of Gandharva marriage).

⚖️ Contrast

  • Core Vedas (Śruti): Both boys and girls had the right to marriage. Girls (brides) were explicitly given the right to choose. Marriage was described as a “saha-dharma” (partnership of equals).
  • Purāṇas / Smṛti: Boys had more freedom of choice, while girls were bound to a guardian-based system. Gandharva marriage (love/mutual choice) was allowed but considered secondary. Overall, there was a tilt towards patriarchal control.

Sati Pratha

1. Core Vedas (Śruti)

  • 🔹 Rigveda 10.18.7–8 (Antyeṣṭi Sūkta):
    • There is a mantra here that is often misunderstood:
    • “Uttishṭhā nāry abhijīva…” → Meaning: “O woman (widow), rise, separate yourself from your husband’s body, and return to the world. Resume your household duties.”
    • It is clear here that the wife is advised to live and start a new life, not to burn herself on the pyre.
  • 🔹 Atharvaveda (18.3.1):
    • This also contains a funeral mantra that mentions the widow returning to life.

➡️ Meaning: In the Śruti (Core Vedas), there is no command for Sati. Instead, widows are encouraged to start a new life.

2. Gṛhya Sūtras & Dharmaśāstras (Smṛti)

  • Baudhāyana Gṛhya Sūtra: Mentions an early tradition where the widow sits beside the pyre, but it is symbolic and does not involve death.
  • Manusmṛti: Does not mandate Sati. However, it imposes very restrictive rules on widows, requiring them to live a life of austerity and discouraging remarriage.

3. Purāṇas

  • Padma Purāṇa, Skanda Purāṇa, Bhaviṣya Purāṇa (medieval interpolations):
    • Sati is glorified as the “highest duty of a devoted wife.”
    • If a widow enters the pyre with her husband, she is promised heavenly rewards.
    • This glorification led to the evolution of the social practice, which became rigid in medieval India.

➡️ Thus, Purāṇic narratives glorified Sati as the ultimate duty of a devoted wife, which became institutionalized in society.

4. Boy vs Girl Perspective

  • Boy (widower, on the death of his wife):
    • No ritual similar to Sati was ever created for him.
    • Widowers were not prohibited from remarrying; in fact, remarriage was encouraged (especially in Manusmṛti).
  • Girl (widow):
    • In the Vedas: Widows are encouraged to live and start a new life.
    • In Smṛti/Purāṇas: Remarriage was discouraged, and Sati was glorified, creating an asymmetric burden on women.

⚖️ Contrast

  • Core Vedas (Śruti): There is no sanction for Sati. Widows are advised to “rise and return to life.”
  • Later Smṛti & Purāṇas: Social restrictions were imposed on widows (Manusmṛti). Purāṇic narratives idealized Sati. Widowers (men) were given freedom, while widows (women) were bound by restrictions.

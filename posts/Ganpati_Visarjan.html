Lord Ganesha

Lord Ganesha (also called Ganapati, Vinayaka, or Vighnaharta) is one of the most widely worshipped deities in Hinduism.

  • Identity: He is the son of Lord Shiva and Goddess Parvati.
  • Appearance: He is easily recognized by his elephant head, large ears, pot-belly, and usually one broken tusk.
  • Symbolism:
    • Elephant head: Wisdom, intelligence, and foresight.
    • Big ears: Ability to listen carefully.
    • Small eyes: Focus and concentration.
    • Broken tusk: Sacrifice and learning (he is said to have written the Mahabharata with it).
  • Role: Known as the remover of obstacles (Vighnaharta), the god of beginnings, wisdom, learning, and prosperity.
  • Worship: Hindus often pray to Ganesha before starting new ventures (business, travel, exams, ceremonies) for success and good fortune.
  • Link to Vedas: The word тАЬGanapatiтАЭ appears in the Rigveda (2.23.1) in a hymn to Bс╣Ыhaspati (Brahmanaspati), the lord of prayers and wisdom:
    тАЬGan─Бn─Бm tv─Б Ganapatim hav─БmaheтАжтАЭ
    Here, тАЬGanapatiтАЭ means тАЬLord of groups/assemblies.тАЭ Later tradition identified this Ganapati with Ganesha.
    • There is no mention of Shiva or Parvati in Vedas.

Ganesh Visarjan

Ganesh Visarjan (рдЧрдгреЗрд╢ рд╡рд┐рд╕рд░реНрдЬрди) is the ritual of immersing the idol of Lord Ganesha in water at the end of the festival Ganesh Chaturthi.

  • Festival Background:
    • Ganesh Chaturthi is a 10-day festival celebrating the birth of Lord Ganesha.
    • Devotees bring clay idols of Ganesha into their homes or public pandals, worship Him with prayers, songs, sweets (especially modak), and rituals.
  • Visarjan (Immersion):
    • On the last day (called Anant Chaturdashi, usually the 10th or sometimes 3rd/5th/7th day depending on tradition), the idol is taken in a procession with music, dance, and chanting of тАЬGanpati Bappa Morya, Pudhchya Varshi Lavkar YaтАЭ (O Lord Ganesha, come back soon next year!).
    • The idol is then immersed in a river, sea, lake, or specially prepared tank.
  • Symbolism:
    • Ganesha is believed to return to Mount Kailash to be with his parents, Shiva and Parvati.
    • Immersion also teaches the spiritual truth of impermanence тАУ everything that takes form eventually merges back into nature. It shows that the form (idol) is temporary, but the spirit (divine presence of Ganesha) is eternal.
    • Eco-friendly clay idols symbolize that we should not harm nature while practicing devotion.
    • It teaches us that God is not confined to one object тАФ He is everywhere, and we can worship Him at any time.
  • Historical Origin of Visarjan:
    • The practice of installing and immersing clay idols of Ganesha as a public festival was started in the late 19th century by Lokmanya Tilak (to unite people during British rule).
    • Before that, Ganesha was mainly worshipped in temples and households, but the immersion ritual was a local/traditional practice. Local Practice likely developed in the medieval period (around 12thтАУ16th century), when Ganesha worship became very popular in western India.
    • This ritual is not mentioned in the Puranas and Vedas.

Water Pollution: Hard Numbers & Impact

Ganesh Visarjan does create significant pollution when idols are made from PoP, synthetic paints, and other non-biodegradable materials. Water quality drastically deteriorates, often surpassing safety thresholds for heavy metals and organic load. The sheer volume of materials (PoP in thousands of tonnes) exacerbates eco-damage.
